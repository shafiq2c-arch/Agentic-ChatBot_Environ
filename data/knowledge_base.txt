### SERVICE: RISING DAMP TREATMENT

**What is rising damp?**
Rising damp is groundwater absorbed upward through porous building materials (brick, mortar) via capillary action — similar to a sponge sitting in a shallow pool of water. It is prevented by a Damp Proof Course (DPC), which has been legally required in all UK houses built since 1875. A failed, bridged, or absent DPC allows water to rise above the habitable level.

**What causes it?**
- Absent or deteriorated DPC (common in pre-1875 buildings)
- Bridged DPC (debris in wall cavity, raised external ground level, patios built above DPC height)
- High groundwater level in the local area
- Porous building materials

**How to identify it:**
- Tide mark / discolouration starting from the floor, usually up to 1 metre
- White powdery salt deposits (efflorescence) at the tide mark line — these are hygroscopic salts that continue absorbing moisture even from the air
- Peeling paint or plaster
- Soft, crumbling plaster
- Damp or peeling skirting boards
- Musty smell
- In extreme cases, standing water on the ground

**Treatment methods Environ uses:**
- **Chemical Injection DPC:** Holes bored into the lowest reachable mortar course, then damp-proofing cream (e.g. Dryzone, Dryrod Damp-Proofing Rods) is injected or manually pumped in. Holes are then plugged with mortar.
- **Physical DPC replacement:** Removal of sections of existing DPC and replacement with a fresh plastic layer or repositioned bricks
- **Damp Proof Membrane (DPM):** Plastic sheeting barrier, often used beneath concrete slabs
- **Re-plastering:** All salt-contaminated plaster must be completely stripped and replaced with salt-resistant plaster to prevent recurring moisture absorption
- **Basement tanking** for severe cases with hydrostatic water pressure

**How long does treatment last?**
Properly treated rising damp should not recur for 15 to 25 years.

**Prevention tips:**
- Ensure DPC is visible at least 15 cm above ground level
- Walk the perimeter regularly to check for DPC breaks
- Maintain gutters and downpipes to avoid overflow near foundations
- Ensure good air circulation in bathrooms and kitchens
- Fix leaks promptly

**DIY vs Professional:**
DIY methods only mask symptoms. Professional treatment identifies the root cause and provides a guaranteed, lasting fix.

### SERVICE: PENETRATING DAMP TREATMENT

**What is penetrating damp?**
Water entering a property through exterior defects — walls, roofs, chimneys, or plumbing. Unlike rising damp, it can occur at any height on the wall.

**Common causes:**
- Cracks in exterior walls (can start hairline and widen through freeze-thaw)
- Cracks around door/window frames that haven't been sealed
- Decayed or poorly installed pointing (mortar between bricks)
- Missing, damaged, or leaking roof tiles
- Damaged chimney flashing, flaunching, or chimney pots
- Blocked or cracked gutters and downspouts
- Leaking plumbing buried in walls
- Subsidence causing structural wall cracks
- Vibration from heavy vehicles or construction widening existing cracks

**Identifying penetrating damp:**
- Damp patches on walls or ceilings (often not directly below the source as water travels along the structure)
- Water stains at any height
- Mould and musty odour
- Peeling paint or plaster
- The site where damp appears is NOT always the site of the leak

**Treatment:**
- Repointing decayed mortar joints
- Crack filling and sealing
- Chimney flashing and flaunching repair
- Gutter and downpipe repair or replacement
- Structural repair of damaged brickwork
- Internal replastering after drying

### SERVICE: LATERAL DAMP TREATMENT

**What is lateral damp?**
Groundwater penetrating sideways into a property through below-ground or basement walls in contact with soil. It can affect any floor-level in contact with earth.

**What causes it?**
- Soil pressing against cracked or porous walls
- Deep basements/cellars (soil is wetter the deeper you dig)
- High groundwater levels (risk is higher in areas near the Thames: Fulham, Putney, Richmond)
- Soil rising over time, exposing walls not built to withstand lateral pressure
- Buildings on sloped ground (additional hydrostatic pressure from water flowing downhill)
- Failed or nonexistent tanking system

**Hygroscopic salt hazard:**
If salts from surrounding soil contaminate the walls, they continuously absorb moisture even from the air — attacking the wall from two fronts simultaneously and rapidly accelerating damp.

**How to identify:**
- Peeling or bubbly paint
- Discoloured and/or crumbling plaster
- Cold, wet walls to the touch
- White, chalky coating (salt deposits)
- Black mould

**Treatment:**
- **Tanking:** Waterproof seal (slurry or membrane) applied to exterior or interior of basement walls to block water at the surface level; allows water to drain back through the exterior
- **Cavity drain membrane + sump pump:** Used when soil is too saturated — a gap is left between the wall and waterproof coating; water collects and is pumped out via a sump pump into the drainage system
- Soil excavation to remove excess build-up against the wall
- Hygroscopic salt treatment and replastering

### SERVICE: CONDENSATION CONTROL & PIV UNIT INSTALLATION

**What is condensation?**
Condensation occurs when warm, moist indoor air contacts a cold surface and the water vapour turns into liquid droplets. It is the most common form of household damp.

**What causes indoor condensation?**
Everyday activities add moisture to the air:
- Cooking
- Showering and bathing
- Drying clothes indoors
- Breathing and sleeping
- Using humidifiers
Modern homes are tightly sealed for energy efficiency, trapping this moisture inside. Period homes lack the active ventilation to expel it.

**Two types affecting buildings:**
1. **Surface condensation:** Visible on windows, walls, floors — most common. Caused by warm moist air meeting a cold surface.
2. **Interstitial condensation:** Hidden inside walls, floors, roofs. Moist air penetrates building materials and condenses within. Causes wood rot, weakened insulation, and structural degradation. Common in insulated cavity walls.

**Signs of condensation problem:**
- Water pooling on windowsills
- Thick droplets forming every morning
- Moisture between double-glazed panes (seal failure — requires unit replacement, not cleaning)
- Black mould around window frames
- Musty smells
- Wallpaper peeling near windows
- Damp patches spreading from windows

**When to worry:**
- Condensation appears all day, not just in the morning
- Water drips onto the sill daily
- Walls around windows feel damp
- Black mould keeps returning despite cleaning
- Windows remain foggy even with ventilation

**Practical solutions (DIY):**
- Open windows for a few minutes each morning
- Use extractor fans in bathrooms and kitchens; keep running after showers or cooking
- Move furniture away from exterior walls for air circulation
- Avoid drying clothes indoors
- Keep a stable room temperature
- Use a dehumidifier in severely affected rooms
- Maintain indoor humidity below 50–55%
- Repair any failed window seals

**Professional solutions Environ provides:**

**PIV (Positive Input Ventilation) Unit Installation:**
- How it works: By constantly ventilating the home with fresh air from the loft or outside, PIV units increase indoor air pressure. Higher indoor pressure pushes stale, moist air out through existing vents and gaps — taking moisture and allergens with it.
- Results: Immediate improvement in air quality; condensation, damp and mould progressively reduced or eliminated entirely
- Works in both houses and flats (wall-mounted between loft and floor below, or on an external wall)
- Cost to install: From £950 + VAT
- Running cost: Pennies a day
- Over 1 million homes in the UK have PIV units installed
- Vent Axia PIV unit is a trusted model Environ uses
- PIV units with air heating available for flats without loft access (slightly higher running cost, offset by reduced heating demand since windows don't need to be opened in winter)

**Note on damp proof paint:**
- Anti-condensation paint only helps if cold surfaces are the *sole* cause of condensation
- Anti-mould paint contains fungicides; temporary fix (lasts up to 5+ years at best)
- Waterproof paint can trap moisture behind it, making damp worse
- NEVER paint over damp as the only fix — root moisture cause must be treated first
- Two coats of damp proof paint required for any barrier effectiveness

### SERVICE: MOULD TREATMENT & REMOVAL / MOULD REMEDIATION

**What is mould?**
A fungal growth requiring moisture, an organic surface, and oxygen. It thrives in damp, warm, dark, poorly ventilated environments. Common species: black mould (Stachybotrys), Aspergillus, Penicillium. Mould emits over 200 types of microbial volatile organic compounds (MVOCs) — responsible for the distinctive musty smell.

**Why black mould is serious:**
- Feeds on cellulose found in wood, carpets, curtains, paint, and plaster
- Spreads spores through the air; you inhale them unknowingly
- Cannot be permanently removed without eliminating the moisture source
- Common in London's Victorian and period homes due to lack of active ventilation + modern lifestyle moisture generation

**Health risks:**
- Nasal stuffiness, throat irritation, coughing, wheezing, eye irritation
- Headaches, dizziness, nausea, fatigue, skin rashes
- Worsened asthma and allergic reactions
- Respiratory infections
- Dangerous long-term exposure for children, elderly, and immunocompromised individuals
- NHS confirms: "If you have damp and mould, you're more likely to have respiratory problems, infections, allergies or asthma"

**Signs you need professional mould removal:**
- Musty or damp smells
- Black or green spots on walls, ceilings, or around windows
- Flaky or bubbling paint
- Water stains on ceilings or walls
- Condensation in corners
- Visible mould on furniture
- Unexplained allergy symptoms
- Mould area exceeds 10 square feet → professional remediation required

**When mould starts forming:**
Within 24–48 hours of moisture lingering on a surface.

**DIY removal (for small patches only):**
- Wear rubber gloves, goggles, and a respirator mask
- Use detergent and water to scrub hard surfaces
- Natural option: distilled white vinegar (diluted)
- Chemical option: 1 cup chlorine bleach per 1 gallon of water (do NOT mix with ammonia)
- Bag and dispose of rags and sponges outdoors
- Throw away any porous/absorbent mouldy materials
- Ensure proper ventilation while cleaning

**Professional Mould Remediation Process (Environ's 7-Step Method):**
1. **Mould Inspection:** Specialist checks affected areas, measures humidity, identifies mould species
2. **Containment:** Seals off the area to prevent spores spreading to other rooms
3. **Air Filtration:** HEPA air scrubbers remove airborne spores during work
4. **Mould Removal:** Contaminated materials that cannot be saved (drywall, insulation, carpet) safely removed
5. **Cleaning & Sanitising:** Walls, floors, ceilings, and HVAC components cleaned with safe antimicrobial methods
6. **Repairs & Restoration:** Damaged areas patched, walls repainted, surfaces restored
7. **Moisture Control Guidance:** Advice on improving airflow, reducing humidity, repairing leaks

**The difference between Mould Removal and Mould Remediation:**
- **Removal** = eliminating visible mould
- **Remediation** = full restoration process: fix moisture source + clean contaminated areas + repair damage + restore to safe state

**Long-term prevention:**
- Use bathroom and kitchen exhaust fans consistently
- Fix leaks immediately
- Keep indoor humidity below 55%
- Open windows for airflow daily
- Use a dehumidifier in damp rooms
- Improve insulation in cold corners
- Clean condensation around windows regularly
- Check basements during rainy seasons
- Air purifiers with HEPA filters eliminate up to 99.97% of airborne particles including mould spores
- Natural odour absorbers: activated charcoal or baking soda (replace every 2 weeks)
- Indoor humidity: ideally 25% in winter and 50% in summer

**Getting rid of mould smell:**
- Identify and eliminate moisture source first
- HEPA air purifiers
- Natural odour absorbers (activated charcoal, baking soda)
- Open windows — fresh air and sunlight both reduce mould and neutralise odours
- Sunlight has natural antimicrobial properties

### SERVICE: DRY ROT CONTROL

**What is dry rot?**
The most aggressive and serious form of wood rot, caused by the fungus *Serpula lacrymans*. Unlike wet rot, dry rot can spread through masonry, across structural timbers, and into areas far removed from the original moisture source.

**Conditions required:**
- Timber moisture content of only ~20% (can develop in less obviously damp conditions)
- Very aggressive once established

**How to identify dry rot:**
- Distinctive musty, damp, earthy smell (often the first sign)
- Wood becomes dry, brittle, and cracked with deep cross-grain cracks
- Timber looks brown and crumbles easily (fungus feeds on the timber's structural elements — brown rot fungi)
- White cotton-wool-like or silky white sheet growth on timber (mycelium)
- Lemon-yellow tinge on fungal growth when exposed to light
- Flat mushroom-like fruiting bodies that can grow through plaster/paint and release spores
- Bouncy, creaky, or sagging floors
- White powdery spore dust

**How dry rot spreads:**
- Thick white fungal strands (hyphae/mycelium) penetrate plaster, brickwork, and hidden timbers
- Can travel far from the original moisture source
- Spreads rapidly if left untreated — the most dangerous form of wood rot

**Testing for dry rot:**
- Prod suspect timber with a screwdriver — dry rot timber crumbles or breaks easily
- Professional inspectors use moisture meters and infrared imaging for hidden areas

**Treatment process:**
1. Remove the source of moisture that enabled fungal growth
2. Remove all infected timber and plaster (including around the affected area)
3. Apply fungicide treatment to all surrounding surfaces
4. Fix any structural integrity issues
5. Replace damaged timber
6. Apply preserved, treated replacement timber
7. Consider tanking if damp source is structural

**If left untreated:**
- Spreads to previously unaffected areas
- Can make a building structurally unsafe
- Spore dust released into the living space poses health risks
- Creates conditions for more widespread wood-destroying fungi

**Dry rot vs wet rot (key differences):**

| Feature | Dry Rot | Wet Rot |
|---|---|---|
| Fungus | Serpula lacrymans | Coniophora puteana, Fibroporia vaillantii |
| Moisture required | ~20% | ~50% |
| Spread | Aggressively through masonry | Confined to damp area |
| Appearance | Brown, crumbling, cross-grain cracks | Soft, spongy, darkened, flaky paint |
| Mycelium | Thick white sheets, penetrates walls | Minimal root growth |
| Danger level | Very high | Moderate |

### SERVICE: WET ROT CONTROL

**What is wet rot?**
A group of fungi that cause timber decay in persistently damp conditions. Less aggressive than dry rot — stays confined to the moisture source area.

**Conditions required:**
- Timber moisture content of ~50% or higher
- Persistent damp, usually from leaks or poor ventilation

**Causes:**
Coniophora puteana (cellar fungus) and Fibroporia vaillantii (mine fungus) are the most common species.

**How to identify:**
- Black fungus on timber
- Timber feels wet and spongy
- Discoloured (often darker) wood
- Damaged paint finish (flaking paint)
- Distinctive damp, musty smell
- Common in window frames and areas with persistent moisture

**How to differentiate from dry rot:**
- Wet rot feels damp and spongy; dry rot feels dry and brittle
- Wet rot stays localised; dry rot spreads aggressively
- Wet rot does NOT produce extensive white root growth

**Consequences of leaving wet rot untreated:**
- Spreads through property's timber over time
- Sagging floors, warped door frames
- Eventually creates the perfect environment for a dry rot outbreak — which is far more destructive
- Releases wood-destroying fungi spores, posing health risks

**Treatment:**
1. Eliminate the moisture source
2. Remove infected and surrounding timber
3. Apply fungicide treatment
4. Replace damaged structural timber as needed

### SERVICE: BRICK REPOINTING

**What is repointing?**
Removing deteriorated mortar between bricks and replacing it with new mortar to restore structural integrity, prevent water ingress, and maintain aesthetic appeal.

**Why it matters:**
- Mortar is the first line of defence against water ingress
- Decayed pointing allows water into the brick via capillary action
- In cold weather, water in bricks freezes, expands, and shatters the brick (freeze-thaw)
- A single damaged brick can weaken an entire wall
- Environ's specialist tip: if a frost-damaged brick has lost its smooth surface, they use a specialist brick cutter to remove and flip the brick — undamaged face outward — effectively doubling its lifespan without replacement

**How often to repoint:**
Mortar typically lasts 40+ years, but factors including harsh weather, salt spray, and high altitudes can accelerate deterioration. Regular inspection is advised.

**Signs your house needs repointing:**
- Cracks in the mortar joints
- Soft or crumbling mortar you can remove with your fingers or a butter knife
- Loose or falling bricks
- Gaps or holes in the brickwork
- Damp patches on walls, especially lower areas
- Moss or algae growing in joints (indicates moisture retention)
- Bricks eroded more than the pointing (sign of harder cement mortar damaging softer bricks)

**Why lime mortar is essential for historic buildings:**
- Flexible — allows the building to shift naturally without cracking
- Breathable — absorbs and releases moisture, preventing trapped damp
- Prevents frost/weather damage
- Can be colour-matched to original mortar
- If mortar is harder than the brick (as with cement), compressive force cracks the bricks instead of compressing the mortar
- Cement mortar is impermeable — traps moisture in bricks, causing freeze-thaw damage and salt crystal growth
- Easy to spot bad cement pointing: bricks are more eroded than the mortar, which is the reverse of correct aging

**Environ's repointing process:**
1. Remove decayed mortar by hand using hammer and chisel (carefully, without damaging bricks)
2. Thoroughly clean gaps of all dust and debris
3. Assess original mortar composition
4. Mix bespoke lime-based mortar to match the original in softness, permeability, and colour
5. Apply starting from top of wall, working downward
6. Fill vertical joints after completing horizontal rows
7. Remove excess mortar with wire brush for a clean finish
8. Final inspection for visual appeal and weather-resistance

**Repointing vs Rendering:**
- Repointing: strengthens brick walls by replacing damaged mortar — better for long-term breathability and structural integrity; lasts 40+ years; lower maintenance
- Rendering: applies a protective layer over masonry — enhances appearance but can be more costly and may trap moisture if not breathable; shorter lifespan

**DIY vs Professional:**
- Small areas can be DIY but requires skill
- For listed buildings or heritage properties, professional is legally required
- Incorrect cement mortar in repointing can cause irreparable damage to period brickwork

**Environ's edge:**
Director Terry Clark (SPAB member) personally oversees all major conservation-grade repointing works. From historic townhouses in Chelsea to modern builds across Greater London.

### SERVICE: BRICK CLEANING

**Types of chemical brick cleaners used:**
- **Acidic cleaners** (e.g. hydrochloric/muriatic acid): Remove mineral deposits, efflorescence, certain stains — must be used carefully to avoid etching brick or eroding mortar
- **Alkaline cleaners:** Dissolve organic matter, grease, and dirt; used after acidic cleaning to neutralise residual acidity
- **Biodegradable/eco-friendly cleaners:** Effective against dirt and stains with minimal environmental impact
- **Specialised stain cleaners:** Target oil, graffiti, rust, smoke damage

**Brick cleaning process (Environ's method):**
1. Identify brick type and assess porosity (high-porosity bricks need gentler methods)
2. Inspect for pre-existing damage (cracks, loose bricks, deteriorated mortar)
3. **Patch test** the cleaner on a small area first — non-negotiable step
4. Remove loose dirt and debris before applying chemicals
5. Apply cleaning solution with appropriate technique (brush, spray, or controlled application)
6. Allow dwell time (minutes to a full day depending on product)
7. Neutralise residual acidity with alkaline solution
8. Rinse thoroughly with clean water
9. Proper drying to prevent moisture-related issues
10. Free follow-up arranged to confirm client satisfaction

**Advanced techniques:**
- Chemical peeling: Deep clean for heavily soiled bricks, lifting ingrained dirt that standard cleaning cannot reach
- Compatible with hot water pressure washing
- Compatible with steam cleaning for exterior surfaces and patios
- Stayntech® brick stains can be applied after cleaning to protect brick while allowing natural evaporation

**Safety measures:**
- PPE mandatory: gloves, goggles, respirator
- Proper chemical storage: cool, dry place, away from incompatible substances
- First aid for exposure: immediate rinsing with clean water + medical advice
- Eco-responsible disposal of waste products per local regulations

**Important warnings:**
- Never apply sealants as a first line of defence without accurate diagnosis
- Off-the-shelf sealants can trap moisture inside walls, accelerating damp
- Sealants do not replace structural remedies (repointing, crack repair, drainage)

**Services covered:**

### SERVICE: CHIMNEY DAMP REPAIR

**Why chimney damp matters:**
Leaky chimneys are one of the most common causes of penetrating damp in roofs. Roof damp is especially dangerous due to the abundance of structural timber, which is at high risk of wet rot, dry rot, wood-boring insects, and mould.

**4 main causes of chimney damp:**

1. **Leaking chimney flashing:** Metal sheets at the junction of chimney and roof tiles. Over time, sheets peel away or the caulking seal erodes. Symptoms: wet, discoloured, or dripping roof timbers; sagging or bubbling plaster in converted lofts.

2. **Damaged chimney stack:** Like any brick wall, the stack is vulnerable to decayed pointing and cracked bricks. Many chimneys have been incorrectly repointed with hard cement mortar — damages brickwork and prevents breathability. Symptoms: penetrating damp, compromised structure.

3. **Cracked chimney flaunching:** The mortar/concrete holding chimney pots in place. Cracks fill with water and widen through freeze-thaw; water trickles into the chimney stack or flues.

4. **Damaged or missing chimney pots/cowls:** Designed to stop draughts and rainwater from entering flues. If damaged or missing, rainwater trickles down flues → increased moisture levels → condensation risk.

**Recommendation:**
Regular chimney inspections are advised — chimneys are often out of sight and can decay severely before being noticed, significantly reducing property value.

## BUYING A PROPERTY — DAMP CHECK GUIDE

**Why it's essential:**
- Damp problems = expensive repairs, structural issues, health risks
- Can cause wooden structures to rot, plaster to crumble, mould to grow
- Ignoring damp during purchase can lead to unforeseen expenses and significantly reduced resale value

**What to look for (visible signs):**
- Mould or mildew around windows and doors
- Discoloured patches on wallpaper
- Peeling/flaking wallpaper or paint
- Crumbling plaster
- Damp patches on walls and ceilings
- Staining and discolouration at floor level (rising damp tide mark)
- White powdery deposits on walls (efflorescence)
- Spongy or rotting skirting boards and floorboards

**Areas to check:**
- Walls and ceilings (peeling, staining, crumbling plaster)
- Skirting boards and floorboards (soft, spongy = moisture damage)
- Basement and cellar (musty smell, cold walls, standing water)
- Behind kitchen appliances and under bathroom sinks
- Around windows and doors

**Hidden damp detection:**
- Moisture meters: measure moisture content in walls and floors (electrical resistance or capacitive measurement)
- Thermal imaging cameras: detect hidden moisture behind walls
- Professional surveyor: mandatory for accurate, unbiased diagnosis

**3 types of damp to know:**
1. **Rising damp:** Tide mark below 1m; efflorescence; peeling skirting boards → DPC issue
2. **Penetrating damp:** Staining at any height; associated with cracks, poor pointing, roof/gutter defects
3. **Condensation:** Mould near windows; musty smell; poor ventilation

**Damp-proofing requirements (building regulations):**
- External walls must sit at least 150mm above ground level
- DPC must be continuous with any damp-proof membrane in the floor
- DPC must be at least 225mm above the bottom of a cavity wall unless a cavity tray is used

## CONDENSATION ON WINDOWS — COMPLETE GUIDE

**Why it happens:**
Warm humid air inside the home meets the cold glass surface. Water vapour cools below dew point and condenses into liquid droplets.

**Most at-risk rooms:**
Bathrooms, kitchens, bedrooms, basements, laundry rooms.

**Condensation between panes of double glazing:**
This is different from surface condensation. It means the window seal has failed and the insulating gas has escaped. The unit is no longer airtight. This requires repair or full replacement of the glass unit — not just cleaning.

**Practical fixes (step by step):**
1. Increase ventilation — open windows daily
2. Use extractor fans in bathrooms and kitchens
3. Move furniture away from windows and cold walls
4. Reduce indoor drying
5. Keep stable room temperature
6. Use a dehumidifier
7. Repair failed window seals
8. Improve wall and window insulation

**Long-term habits for a dry home:**
- Keep rooms ventilated daily
- Repair leaks promptly
- Use extractor fans consistently
- Avoid blocking vents
- Monitor indoor humidity levels
- Check windows regularly for mould and damp patches
- Consider energy-efficient glazing

## COMPLETE FAQ (FROM ALL BLOG CONTENT)

**Q: What is the difference between wet rot and dry rot?**
A: Wet rot feels soft, spongy, and damp; confined to moist areas. Dry rot is dry, brittle, cracked, and spreads aggressively through masonry and timbers beyond the moisture source.

**Q: How do I test for dry rot?**
A: Prod suspect timber with a screwdriver — dry rot timber crumbles easily. Professionals use moisture meters and infrared imaging for hidden areas.

**Q: How do I identify rising damp?**
A: Look for a tide mark (discolouration starting at the floor, stopping at ~1m), white powdery salt deposits, peeling paint, crumbling plaster, and damp skirting boards.

**Q: What is lateral damp?**
A: Groundwater seeping sideways into basement or below-ground walls. More common in areas near the Thames.

**Q: Can I just paint over damp walls?**
A: No. Painting over damp is a temporary measure at best. The root moisture source must be fixed first. Painting over damp allows it to continue spreading beneath the surface.

**Q: How long does rising damp treatment last?**
A: 15 to 25 years when properly treated with a chemical or physical DPC.

**Q: What causes condensation in my home?**
A: Warm, moist indoor air contacts cold surfaces. Cooking, showering, drying clothes indoors, and poor ventilation all contribute.

**Q: Is condensation dangerous?**
A: Yes, ongoing condensation causes black mould, which triggers allergies, asthma, and respiratory infections. It also causes structural damage.

**Q: How do PIV units work?**
A: PIV units create positive indoor air pressure by continuously supplying fresh air from the loft or outside, pushing stale, moist air out through existing vents and gaps.

**Q: How much does a PIV unit installation cost?**
A: From £950 + VAT, installed by Environ. Running cost is pennies a day.

**Q: What are the signs of damp before buying a house?**
A: Mould around windows/doors, discoloured wallpaper, peeling paint, crumbling plaster, musty smells, spongy floorboards or skirting boards, tide marks below 1m on walls.

**Q: How often should I repoint my house?**
A: Approximately every 40+ years, but regular inspections are essential to catch early deterioration.

**Q: What are signs my house needs repointing?**
A: Cracks in mortar joints, crumbling mortar you can remove by hand, loose bricks, damp patches on walls, moss or algae in joints.

**Q: Why is lime mortar essential for historic buildings?**
A: It is flexible, breathable, and softer than brick — allowing natural movement and moisture release. Cement mortar damages historic bricks by trapping moisture and resisting natural movement.

**Q: Can I repoint my house myself?**
A: Small areas can be DIY, but larger or heritage projects require professional expertise to avoid permanent damage.

**Q: What causes black mould in London homes?**
A: London's older period properties lack active ventilation, and modern lifestyles (showering, cooking, drying clothes indoors) add far more moisture than these buildings were designed to handle. Heavy-handed insulation blocking natural airflow makes it worse.

**Q: How do I get rid of the smell of mould?**
A: Eliminate the moisture source first, then use HEPA air purifiers, natural odour absorbers (activated charcoal, baking soda), open windows for sunlight and fresh air.

**Q: Is mould removal a DIY job?**
A: Small patches can be cleaned with bleach solution or vinegar. Areas larger than 10 sq ft, or hidden mould inside walls, require professional mould remediation.

**Q: What is mould remediation vs mould removal?**
A: Removal eliminates visible mould. Remediation is a full restoration: fix moisture source, clean all contaminated areas, repair damage, restore the space so mould cannot return.

**Q: What is chemical brick cleaning?**
A: Using specially formulated acidic, alkaline, biodegradable, or specialist chemical solutions to remove stains, efflorescence, biological growth, and pollution deposits from brick surfaces.

**Q: Why is a patch test important before brick cleaning?**
A: To confirm the cleaner is compatible with the specific brick type and condition, preventing widespread damage or adverse reactions.

**Q: How does chimney damp enter a property?**
A: Through damaged flashing (junction of chimney and roof), decayed chimney stack pointing, cracked flaunching (mortar holding chimney pots), or missing/damaged chimney pots and cowls.

**Q: What is a damp proof course (DPC)?**
A: A layer of water-resistant material built into walls (legally required since 1875) to prevent rising damp from travelling upward through masonry.

**Q: What is tanking?**
A: A waterproof seal (slurry or membrane) applied to basement walls to block water from reaching the interior. Used for lateral and basement damp.

**Q: What is Environ's Total Envelope Protocol™?**
A: A 3-stage proprietary system: Stage 1 — Forensic diagnosis; Stage 2 — Dry and stabilise; Stage 3 — Treat and restore with appropriate materials and guarantees.

**Q: Are Georgian sash windows worth repairing?**
A: Yes. Georgian sash windows last 200+ years, and original Georgian glass is irreplaceable. Repairing is always preferable to replacing with uPVC.

**Q: What are Edwardian houses prone to?**
A: Decayed pointing, blown brickwork, rotten timbers, missing roof tiles, and damp problems — especially when walls have been improperly sealed, insulated, or rendered, blocking natural breathability.

**Q: What are the health risks of wood rot?**
A: Spores from dry rot and wet rot fungi aggravate asthma and respiratory conditions. In severe structural cases, untreated rot can make a building unsafe for occupation.

**Q: How do I avoid damp scams?**
A: Use companies that ask detailed questions before diagnosis, provide a written survey report with photographic evidence, explain findings clearly, offer long-term guarantees (5–25 years), and never pressure you into an immediate decision.

**Q: What does a damp survey include?**
A: Moisture readings, thermal imaging, moisture migration mapping, photographic documentation of ingress points, a written report within 48 hours, and a recommended treatment plan.
